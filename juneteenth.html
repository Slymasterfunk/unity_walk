Juneteenth

Juneteenth, also known as Freedom Day or Emancipation Day, is an annual holiday celebrated on June 19th. The holiday commemorates the emancipation of enslaved African Americans and serves as a day of reflection and celebration of freedom.


On June 19, 1865, Union General Gordon Granger arrived in Galveston, Texas, and announced General Order No. 3, which proclaimed the freedom of enslaved people in Texas, making them the last group of slaves to be emancipated in the United States. This announcement came more than two years after the Emancipation Proclamation was issued by President Abraham Lincoln in 1863.


Juneteenth is often marked by various events and activities highlighting African American history and culture. These include parades, festivals, picnics, barbecues, music performances, educational programs, and community gatherings. It is a time for reflection on the struggles and achievements of African Americans throughout history, as well as a celebration of the principles of freedom, equality, and unity.


Recently, there has been a growing movement to recognize Juneteenth as a national holiday. On June 17, 2021, it was officially declared a federal holiday in the United States when President Joe Biden signed the Juneteenth National Independence Day Act into law. This designation acknowledges the historical significance of Juneteenth and its importance in the country's ongoing journey toward racial equality and justice.
